01 — The Insight

What is the tension in the air?

"Ghana grows the tomato. Ghana cannot afford the tomato. The soil is ours. The tin is not."
The Core Tension — Campaign Insight Statement

Ghana is one of West Africa's largest tomato-producing nations. The Northern Region and Brong Ahafo produce millions of tonnes of fresh tomatoes every year. Yet the markets of Accra, Kumasi, and Takoradi are stacked floor to ceiling with tins of imported tomato paste from China, Italy, and Turkey.

The tension is not a supply problem. It is a story problem. Ghana has been told by the structure of its own markets that the product of its own soil is not good enough to reach its own kitchen. A Ghanaian mother in Accra has never made a connection between the tomato field in Bawku and the paste in her pot. That disconnection is the wound this campaign heals.

The National Tomato Production Strategy does not ask Ghana to buy local out of patriotic duty. It asks Ghana to recognise what was always already theirs. This campaign does not build a market. It restores a memory.

The Economic Wound

Ghana spends hundreds of millions of dollars in foreign exchange annually on imported tomato paste while domestic tomatoes rot at the farm gate for lack of cold chain and processing infrastructure. The farmer loses. The consumer pays more. The nation bleeds.

The Cultural Wound

Ghanaian cuisine is built on tomato. Jollof, kontomire, palava sauce, red red, groundnut soup. Every national dish begins with a tomato. Yet the tin it comes from bears a foreign flag. The pride of the kitchen is funded by the humiliation of the farm.

The Resolution

A Made in Ghana tomato processing ecosystem that connects the Northern farmer to the Accra kitchen. The campaign makes that connection visible, sacred, and non-negotiable. When a mother sees the label, she sees the farmer. That is sovereignty.

02 — The Narrative Hook

Find the human pulse.

Two women. Two worlds. One soil.

The first woman is Abena. She is sixty-three. She farms four acres in the Upper East Region. She has farmed tomatoes since her hands were the size of her daughter's palms. She does not know where her harvest goes after the lorry takes it. She has heard it rots. She keeps farming because the soil is her name.

The second woman is Akosua. She is thirty-one. She cooks in a two-room flat in Nungua, Accra. She buys the imported tin every week because it is what is on the shelf. She has never thought to ask where it came from. Her daughter watches her cook. The daughter is learning the world.

The campaign is the moment these two women discover each other. The moment Akosua holds a tin and reads: Packed from tomatoes grown in Upper East Region, Ghana. The moment Abena's hands and Akosua's hands are revealed to be doing the same work from opposite ends of the same country. That moment of recognition is the film.

03 — The Tone & Posture

When do we use the Thunder? When the Whisper?

Deploy Thunder When

The Thunder

National pride demands assertion. Use the Thunder for outdoor billboards on the Accra Tema motorway, for the opening title frame of the flagship film, for radio proclamations at dawn. The Thunder does not ask for permission. It declares. It says: This soil is ours. This red is ours. There is no debate. The Thunder uses full frames of sanguine red. It uses silence before the声 voice. It is sparse. It uses weight, not volume.

Deploy Whisper When

The Whisper

Human truth requires intimacy. Use the Whisper for the close-up of the farmer's hands. For the social media content at 6am when the city is still waking. For the school activation videos. The Whisper does not compete for attention. It waits. It says: Come close. Look at this woman's hands. Look what she has grown for you. The Whisper uses dusty gold light and long focal lengths. It trusts the image to carry the weight.

No wardrobe. Object only. The tin is the subject. Human absence is intentional.

EXTREME CLOSE-UP. A tin of imported tomato paste on a supermarket shelf in Accra. The label is in a foreign language. The can is slightly dented. The fluorescent light overhead creates a blue-white highlight across the tin's surface. The image is static. Nothing moves. The world is still and wrong.

AUDIO: Near silence. The low electrical hum of the supermarket refrigerator units. Nothing human.

V.O. (ABENA — BARELY AUDIBLE, AS IF FROM VERY FAR AWAY)
"The lorry came. And then the lorry went."

CUT TO BLACK on the word "went."

Scene 02 of 07
THE FIELD — Upper East Region, Northern Ghana
Timecode: 00:05 — 00:14 · 9 seconds
Emotional Tone
Abundance. Dignity. Ancient. The earth does not apologise.
Dominant Colour Bias
Sanguine Red (#C0392B) against Ochre soil (#B8860B)
Contrast Strategy
Maximum contrast. The red tomatoes against the ochre earth. High saturation. The image should feel almost painterly. Shot with a polarising filter to deepen the sky to cobalt.
Lighting Temperature
2800K — Golden hour. 6:15am. The sun is not yet overhead. The light is almost orange. Long shadows across the rows of plants.
Key Colour Anchors
Tomato red Ochre soil Leaf green Cobalt sky
Wardrobe Direction
No human in frame yet. Wide establishing shot of the field only. Let the crop breathe.

WIDE AERIAL SHOT — a drone rises slowly from the ochre earth between the tomato rows, lifting to reveal an enormous field of red tomatoes at full harvest. The field extends to the horizon. The sky is deep cobalt. The contrast is savage and beautiful. This is abundance. This is Ghana.

AUDIO: The sound arrives before the image fully resolves — a woman's voice, humming. Traditional Northern Ghanaian work song, a cappella. No instruments. Just the voice and the field.

The drone continues rising until the field becomes a pattern of red and ochre geometry against the earth. Hold for two seconds on the wide shot.

Scene 03 of 07
THE FARMER — Abena, Upper East Region
Timecode: 00:14 — 00:26 · 12 seconds
Emotional Tone
Sacred. Intimate. The weight of a life of work. Pride that does not perform.
Dominant Colour Bias
Dusty Gold skin tones against Sanguine Red tomatoes
Contrast Strategy
The face is lit from the side by the golden hour sun. The shadow side falls into warm sienna, not cold grey. There is no fill light. The contrast is earned by the sun alone.
Lighting Temperature
2800K key light from screen right (sun). No reflector. No fill. Natural only. The shadow should go to #3D2B1F (Dark Earth) — never grey.
Key Colour Anchors
Deep indigo smock Gold headwrap Tomato in hand Soil on hands
Wardrobe Direction
ABENA wears deep indigo smock (Ghanaian smock fabric, not generic). Gold headwrap, not tied — slightly loose. Hands bare. Significant soil on the hands. No jewellery except a single bead bracelet at the wrist. The indigo against the ochre earth is the critical frame.

CLOSE-UP on ABENA's hands. She lifts a ripe tomato from the vine. Her hands are the hands of sixty years of farming. The soil is in the lines of her knuckles. She turns the tomato over. Examines it the way a jeweller examines a stone. She knows what this is worth.

CUT TO: MEDIUM SHOT. ABENA's face in profile against the field. The morning light falls across her cheekbone. She does not look at camera. She looks at the tomato. Then at the horizon.

ABENA (in Gurene, subtitled)
"My mother's mother planted this same soil. The soil does not forget us."

She places the tomato gently into the harvest basket. Her hands linger on it for one beat longer than necessary.

Scene 04 of 07
THE KITCHEN — Akosua's Flat, Nungua, Accra
Timecode: 00:26 — 00:34 · 8 seconds
Emotional Tone
Ordinary. Not sad. This is not tragedy yet — it is simply incomplete. The disconnection is quiet, not dramatic.
Dominant Colour Bias
Harmattan Dust (#E8D5A3) walls / imported tin silver — the warmth is muted
Contrast Strategy
Lower contrast than Scenes 2 and 3. The image is slightly washed. This is the middle of the story, not its resolution. Do not grade warmly yet.
Lighting Temperature
2200K — Gas flame under the pot. Morning kitchen window light at 4500K mixing with the gas flame warmth. The conflict of temperatures reflects the disconnection in the story.
Key Colour Anchors
Kitchen wall Imported tin Gas flame Clay pot
Wardrobe Direction
AKOSUA in a warm brown batakari print housecoat — warm but muted. Her daughter wears school uniform, white blouse. The daughter's presence is critical: she is watching. She is learning this act.

AKOSUA at the stove. She opens the imported tin with a tin-opener. The sound of the metal is sharp and cold — a deliberately unnatural intrusion into the warmth of the kitchen. She pours the paste into the pot. Her young daughter sits at the table behind her, watching.

CLOSE-UP: The tin's label. Foreign language text. The bright colour of it does not belong to the kitchen's warmth.

CLOSE-UP: The daughter's eyes. Watching her mother's hands. Learning the ritual of the meal — but not yet the whole story.

AUDIO: The sound design drops the kitchen ambient briefly. One beat of silence. The story holds its breath.

Scene 05 of 07
THE TRANSFORMATION — Tomato Processing Facility, Ghana
Timecode: 00:34 — 00:44 · 10 seconds
Emotional Tone
Industry as dignity. The factory floor is not cold here. It is the bridge. It is where the farmer's hands and the mother's pot finally connect.
Dominant Colour Bias
Sanguine Red machinery / Bone White uniforms / Dusty Gold overhead light
Contrast Strategy
The saturation returns here after Scene 4's muted register. The tomatoes on the conveyor belt are gloriously, almost aggressively red. This is the return of colour.
Lighting Temperature
3200K — Warm industrial overhead. Not cold factory light. The warmth signals that this factory belongs to the Ghana Soil System, not to the import register.
Key Colour Anchors
Tomato flow Worker uniform Overhead warm Made in Ghana label
Wardrobe Direction
Workers in bone white uniforms with a sanguine red collar trim. Ghanaian flag pin on each uniform. The uniforms read as pride, not anonymous labour. One worker is clearly from the North — the cultural continuity between the farm and the factory is visible in the face.

WIDE SHOT of the processing floor. Tomatoes flow on a conveyor belt in a river of red. Ghanaian workers in white uniforms observe the flow, adjust the machinery, inspect the product. The pace is measured. The work is deliberate.

CLOSE-UP: A tin moving along a labelling machine. The label is applied. The camera follows the tin as it moves forward. We do not yet see the label clearly.

V.O. (ABENA, in Gurene, subtitled)
"I did not know where the lorry went. Now I know. It came home."
Scene 06 of 07
THE RECOGNITION — Accra Supermarket
Timecode: 00:44 — 00:52 · 8 seconds
Emotional Tone
Discovery. A quiet revolution at the level of a shelf. The world shifts in the space of reading a label.
Dominant Colour Bias
Sanguine Red label / Dusty Gold lettering — the palette has fully arrived
Contrast Strategy
Return to Scene 1's location. Same shelf. Same fluorescent light angle. But this tin is different. The contrast between the old cold tin (Scene 1) and this warm red tin is the film's visual thesis statement.
Lighting Temperature
3800K — The fluorescent lights are the same, but Akosua's warm skin tones and the red label pull the scene warm despite the cold source. Grade corrects warmly in post.

Wardrobe Direction
AKOSUA in the same housecoat as Scene 4 — but this time her daughter is with her at the supermarket. The daughter reaches forward and picks up the tin first. The child sees it before the adult. This is intentional.

AKOSUA and her daughter in the supermarket aisle. The daughter reaches forward and picks up the Ghanaian-made tin. She holds it out to her mother. Akosua takes it. Turns it over.

EXTREME CLOSE-UP on the label. We read it clearly for the first time: "PACKED FROM TOMATOES GROWN IN UPPER EAST REGION, GHANA."

CLOSE-UP on Akosua's face. She does not smile immediately. She looks at the label for a long moment. Then she looks at her daughter. Then she puts the foreign tin back on the shelf. She keeps the Ghanaian tin.

AUDIO: The work song from Scene 2 returns, faintly. The two sounds — the kitchen and the field — are beginning to occupy the same space.

Primary headline: Playfair Display Black, uppercase, tight tracking. Sub-line: Space Mono, light weight. No serif on sub-line. The contrast of old-world serif and modern mono is intentional: heritage and future, simultaneously.
